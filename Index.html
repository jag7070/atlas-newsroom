What The Latest Green Card Decision Means For International Travel

By Atlas Newsroom Staff • Published

A new U.S. Supreme Court ruling is drawing attention from green card holders and their families because it addresses how some lawful permanent residents can be treated when returning to the United States from international travel. The decision does not change the basic right of most green card holders to travel abroad and return, but it could affect a narrower group of permanent residents who face certain criminal allegations.

In a 6-3 ruling issued June 23, the Supreme Court sided with the federal government in a case involving Muk Choi Lau, a lawful permanent resident who was placed on immigration parole after returning from a trip abroad while facing allegations connected to a counterfeiting offense. The central question was whether border officials needed to meet a higher evidentiary standard before treating a returning green card holder as someone seeking admission to the country rather than simply resuming permanent resident status. The Court concluded that immigration officers were not required to establish by clear and convincing evidence that a crime had been committed before taking that step.

For most green card holders, the ruling is unlikely to alter day-to-day travel plans. Lawful permanent residents generally remain permitted to leave the United States and return, provided they continue to comply with existing immigration requirements and do not abandon their U.S. residence. Current guidance from U.S. Customs and Border Protection regarding routine international travel remains in place.

The decision is most relevant to permanent residents who fall within specific categories already identified in immigration law, particularly those accused of certain criminal offenses. According to court documents and reporting on the case, the ruling concerns the authority of border officials when evaluating returning residents who may be subject to immigration consequences based on alleged criminal conduct.

Legal observers note that the practical effect could be greater discretion for border officials in some cases involving returning permanent residents. Individuals who are under indictment, facing certain criminal allegations, or involved in proceedings that could affect admissibility may receive additional scrutiny when reentering the country. However, the ruling does not create a new category of travelers subject to inspection, nor does it automatically revoke anyone’s green card. Immigration proceedings and other legal processes would still determine any final outcome regarding status.

What remains unchanged is equally important. The decision does not affect U.S. citizens, does not alter the legal status of most green card holders, and does not eliminate established travel rights for lawful permanent residents. Travelers who maintain their residence in the United States and comply with immigration rules generally continue to be eligible for reentry after temporary trips abroad.

Looking ahead, immigration attorneys, advocacy groups, and government agencies are expected to study how the ruling is applied at ports of entry. Additional agency guidance, future court cases, or policy clarifications could further define the decision’s impact. For now, the ruling is best understood as a case about the government's authority in a limited set of circumstances involving certain returning permanent residents—not a broad change to international travel rules for all green card holders.
